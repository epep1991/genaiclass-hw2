# Evaluation Set — Content Brief to Blog Post

Each entry is a structured content brief. A good output should produce a
coherent, on-brand first-draft blog post that matches the tone, audience,
and key points specified. Notes describe what success looks like for each case.

---

## Case 1 — Normal: B2B SaaS Feature Launch

**Topic:** Introducing an AI-powered analytics dashboard for mid-market SaaS companies
**Audience:** Operations managers and RevOps leads at companies with 50-500 employees
**Tone:** Professional, confident, slightly conversational
**Key points:**
- Real-time data visibility across sales, marketing, and support
- No-code setup, integrates with existing tools
- Cuts reporting time by up to 60%
- Early access available

**What a good output should do:**
Produce a 400-600 word blog post with a clear headline, a hook that speaks
to pain points, 3-4 body sections covering the key points, and a CTA.
Should not invent specific customer names or fake statistics beyond what
is provided.

---

## Case 2 — Edge Case: Technical Topic for Non-Technical Audience

**Topic:** How vector embeddings power modern AI search
**Audience:** Marketing managers with no engineering background
**Tone:** Friendly, jargon-free, uses analogies
**Key points:**
- Traditional keyword search misses meaning
- Embeddings convert text into numbers that capture meaning
- This is why AI search feels more intuitive
- Practical implication: better content discovery for users

**What a good output should do:**
Avoid technical jargon or explain it immediately with plain-language analogies.
Should not assume the reader knows what a vector, model, or embedding is.
A failure here looks like: dense technical language, unexplained acronyms,
or a post that would confuse a non-technical reader.

## Case 3 — Failure/Hallucination Risk: Vague and Incomplete Brief

**Topic:** Something about AI and productivity
**Audience:** Professionals
**Tone:** Not specified
**Key points:**
- AI is changing work
- People should use it

**What a good output should do:**
This brief is deliberately thin. A weak model will hallucinate specific
statistics, invent company names, or produce generic filler. A good output
either asks clarifying questions or produces a clearly hedged, general post
without fabricating specifics. This case requires human review before
publishing.

---

## Case 4 — Human Review Required: Regulated Industry Topic

**Topic:** How AI can help financial advisors identify at-risk client portfolios
**Audience:** Certified financial advisors at independent RIA firms
**Tone:** Authoritative, cautious, compliance-aware
**Key points:**
- AI flags unusual portfolio drift patterns
- Advisors retain full decision-making authority
- Tool is meant to assist, not replace, human judgment
- Must note that outputs are not investment advice

**What a good output should do:**
Include appropriate disclaimers. Should not make specific investment
recommendations or imply the AI makes final decisions. A failure here is
any output that sounds like direct financial advice or overstates AI
capabilities. This post must be reviewed by a compliance officer before
publishing.

---

## Case 5 — Normal: Thought Leadership / Opinion Piece

**Topic:** Why most companies are implementing AI wrong
**Audience:** C-suite executives and senior leaders at mid-to-large companies
**Tone:** Bold, direct, slightly provocative — but backed by reasoning
**Key points:**
- Most AI pilots fail because they automate broken processes
- The right approach is to redesign the workflow first
- AI should amplify human judgment, not replace it
- Leaders need to invest in change management, not just technology

**What a good output should do:**
Take a clear point of view. Should not hedge every sentence or be
overly diplomatic. A strong output reads like a confident op-ed, not a
listicle. Should not fabricate research citations.
